Decisive Insight

Users don't want another meditation app. They need a living soundtrack — one that senses their rhythm, adapts to their emotional state, and removes the friction of choosing the right sound at the right time.

Across 15 interviews totaling 13.96 hours, a single pattern emerged: the gap between users and sound-based wellness isn't about content quality — it's about contextual automation. Users already believe sound regulates their state. They just can't sustain the habit because every session requires manual effort — searching, choosing, adjusting — in moments when their cognitive bandwidth is already depleted.

01 — Problem

Sound wellness tools fail where users need them most

PeopleBio, a Korean biotech company specializing in early neurodegenerative disease detection, wanted to explore binaural beats as a wellness intervention. The market was saturated with sleep and focus apps, yet user retention remained low and services felt interchangeable. They engaged TXGroup to uncover why.

The core question: if binaural beats and music genuinely help users manage sleep, focus, and anxiety, why do they stop using them?

02 — Evidence

What 15 interviews revealed

Interviews surfaced two distinct user archetypes — not by demographics, but by how users regulate their daily rhythm. This framing became the lens for every subsequent design decision.

"I try to set my environment for focus — the right lighting, the right sound — but my schedule changes every day. By the time I find the right track, I've lost the moment." — Participant 03, 39F, Freelance Designer
"My routine is my religion. The same playlist every night. But if something breaks the pattern, I spiral. I just need it to happen automatically." — Participant 05, 43M, Business Owner
Finding 01

Two archetypes, one need

"Routine-based Focusers" maintain rigid daily rhythms and need sound automation that never breaks their flow. "Spontaneous Immersion-seekers" have fluid schedules and need adaptive sound that reads their current state. Both converge on the same core need: reducing the cognitive load of choosing sound.

Finding 02

Sleep quality ↔ Anxiety is a feedback loop

70%+ of participants reported moderate-to-high anxiety. Poor sleep quality led to increased music dependency for anxiety relief, which paradoxically disrupted sleep patterns further. The intervention point isn't sleep or focus — it's the cycle between them.

Finding 03

Sound is already a self-regulation tool

Users don't treat music as background noise. They actively use it to transition between mental states — work-mode to rest, anxiety to calm, drowsiness to alertness. White noise, café sounds, and rhythmic music serve functional, not aesthetic, purposes.

Finding 04

Existing apps feel mechanical, not human

Multiple participants described current wellness apps as "robotic" and "soulless." YouTube was preferred despite worse UX because creators provided a sense of human presence and emotional connection that clinical apps lacked.

04 — Design Decision

KANO-driven feature prioritization

Using the KANO model, I mapped user needs into three tiers to ensure the MVP focused on foundational expectations before investing in delight features. This prevented the common trap of building flashy personalization without solving basic friction first.

Must-Be — Baseline expectations

Frictionless, interruption-free listening

No ads, no jarring transitions, no earphone fatigue. Minimize search fatigue through automated curation. Ensure psychological safety through consistent ambient background. Users expect these by default — their absence causes dissatisfaction, but their presence doesn't delight.

Performance — Satisfaction scales linearly

Personalized sound matching + routine-based auto-play

Sound recommendations adapt to emotional state, focus level, and fatigue. Routine schedules auto-trigger playlists at the right time. The better the personalization and the more reliable the automation, the higher user satisfaction — proportionally.

Excitement — Unexpected delight

Situational automation + emotion-responsive music

TPO (time/place/occasion) context-switching where sound adapts as the user's environment changes. Emotion card interactions that help users identify and express feelings before the service responds. These features create "wow" moments that differentiate from every competitor.

05 — Core Features

Five features, each traced to a pain point

FeaturePain Point → Solution
TPO Auto Scheduler Existing apps require manual setup every session → Auto-play customized sound based on time, location, and calendar-based routine detection.
Focus Mode Widget High barrier to entering flow state → Multiple real-time focus modes accessible via a quick-launch widget, reducing transition time.
Emotion Card System Chronic anxiety with no self-awareness tools → Card-based emotional expression that helps users recognize and articulate their state before receiving matched sound.
Sleep Auto-Setting Sleep quality degradation and wakefulness → Automatically configure sleep-optimized sound environments at the user's set bedtime.
Social Sharing Mechanical, lonely service experience → Routine and experience sharing with comment-based feedback loops to build emotional connection.

Trade-offs Considered

What we prioritized

Contextual automation (TPO + routine detection) over content volume. An emotion card system that creates self-awareness before serving content, rather than a passive recommendation engine. Human warmth in UX language over clinical precision.

What we deprioritized

Biometric hardware integration (wearable heart rate sensors) — too high a barrier for MVP despite strong user interest. Community features scoped as post-launch. Gamification mechanics rejected as incongruent with the calm, wellness-first tone.

06 — Design Direction

From research to concept

Five concept keywords emerged from synthesis: Emotional Flow, Focus Orbit, Flow Sanctuary, Inner Sync, and Mind Alignment. The winning direction — Inner Sync — best captured the service's core promise: real-time synchronization between the user's internal emotional state and their sound environment.

The visual and interaction design drew from serene, warm interior aesthetics — spaces that feel lived-in rather than clinical. UI prototypes featured soft gradients, natural color palettes, and breathing animations that mirror the rhythm of binaural beat frequencies.

Outcome

Research delivered. Design phase in progress.

The research deliverable — including the KANO model, persona frameworks, user journey maps, and feature specifications — was accepted by PeopleBio as the foundation for the UX/UI prototyping phase (Dec 2025 – Mar 2026). The team is now translating these insights into high-fidelity screens and a design system.

07 — Reflection

What I learned

This project reinforced that the most powerful design insight is often about when and how a service enters the user's life — not just what it offers. Users already believed in binaural beats. The bottleneck was contextual: the right sound at the wrong moment is just noise.

Conducting 15 in-depth interviews in Korean across age groups sharpened my ability to synthesize qualitative data into actionable frameworks. The KANO model proved especially valuable as a communication tool with the client — it translated subjective user feedback into a prioritization language that engineering and business stakeholders could immediately act on.
