The product

Five product areas, one coherent experience

Maka Health is a health optimization platform that connects users with practitioners, wellness content, and a token-based reward economy — all in one app. My job was to design the experience that makes this ecosystem feel coherent, usable, and motivating.

This isn't a traditional patient portal. It's a health optimization network — and that distinction shaped every design decision. Users come here to improve, not to manage illness.

Practitioners
Book consultations
Connect with naturopathic physicians, coaches, and dietitians — both virtual and in-person.
Routes
Follow health paths
Personalized wellness paths like "Increase Protein Intake" or "Yoga Meditation" with actionable steps.
Marketplace
Browse supplements & services
Recommended by match percentage — supplements and exclusive services personalized to each user.
Maka Economy
Earn through healthy behaviors
Earn MakaCoin, maintain a MakaScore, and use tokens for marketplace purchases and practitioner bookings.

The core design challenge wasn't any single screen — it was making five distinct product areas feel like one cohesive experience.

Five product areas — Home, Marketplace, Practitioners, Video Library, Maka Economy
Research

Strong point solutions, but no integrated ecosystem

I audited five existing health and wellness platforms — Noom, Headspace, Parsley Health, Hims/Hers, and a smaller direct competitor in the functional medicine space. Most wellness apps excelled at one thing but none combined practitioner access, marketplace, content, and a reward economy in a single experience.

The opportunity for Maka Health was clear — but the risk was equally obvious: trying to do everything often means doing nothing well. The competitive landscape actually made me more conservative about what to ship in v1, not less.

Competitive analysis — Noom, Headspace, Parsley Health, Hims/Hers vs Maka Health
Key findings

What 40+ users and 8 in-depth interviews revealed

I designed a research questionnaire distributed to 40+ potential users in the target demographic — health-conscious adults aged 25–55 who were already spending on wellness but managing it all through disconnected tools and apps. From the responses, I selected 8 participants for in-depth interviews.

Finding 01
Health isn't one category
Users mentally separated fitness, nutrition, and medical consultations into different buckets. The app needed to feel unified without forcing everything into a single view — leading to the five-tab navigation structure.
Finding 02
"I forgot it existed"
Most participants had tried 2–3 wellness apps and abandoned them within a month. The #1 reason was forgetting. The calendar-first Home screen and MakaCoin reward system became retention mechanisms.
Finding 03
Trust is the booking barrier
Users wanted credentials, specializations, and personalized recommendations — not a random directory. This shaped practitioner cards with match percentages, experience years, and free consultation offers.
Core insight
Users want a guide, not a tool
They wanted something that felt like having a health-savvy friend who knows what you need, connects you with the right people, and notices when you're making progress.

Decision 01

Calendar-first, not dashboard-first

Early wireframes tested three Home screen concepts: (A) a health dashboard with MakaScore front-and-center, (B) a content feed showing Routes and video recommendations, and (C) a calendar-first view with today's commitments. Internal testing with the team favored option A, but I pushed for option C based on the research finding that users abandon apps they "forget about."

A calendar with today's scheduled call and training session creates urgency — you have something to show up for. Dashboards are interesting to look at once; calendars create daily habits.

What I prioritized
Weekly calendar showing scheduled activities — discovery calls, cardio training — as the first thing users see
Health Routes and actionable next steps directly below the calendar
Daily urgency and habit-forming touchpoints over passive data display
What I deprioritized
MakaCoin balance sits below the fold — visible if you scroll, not the hero element
MakaScore lives in the Maka Economy tab, not the Home screen
Leading with the token economy would make the app feel transactional rather than health-focused
Home screen — calendar-first layout with annotations
Decision 02

Making the consultation feel accessible

I structured the practitioner profile to answer the three questions users had before booking, in order of priority: "Is this person qualified?" (credentials + experience), "Can I afford it?" (MakaCoin pricing upfront), and "What if I don't like them?" (15-min free consultation as a risk reducer).

The free consultation was the business's idea, but I advocated for making it the most visually prominent element on the profile card — more prominent than the bio — because the research showed that commitment anxiety was the primary booking barrier.

Practitioner list and detail view with booking flow
Decision 03

Making a token system understandable

Token economies are abstract. Users need to understand: What is MakaCoin? How do I earn it? What can I do with it? Why should I care?

I designed the Maka Economy as three progressive layers of understanding: Wallet (what you have — MakaScore gauge, badge tier, coin balance), Activate (how to earn — checklist of qualifying actions with clear criteria), and My Voice (why it matters — community participation and governance).

The MakaScore gauge was a deliberate choice over a simple number. In testing, participants responded more strongly to the visual gauge — which reads like a "health credit score" — than to a plain token count. The "+20 Biweekly Trend" indicator was my addition: it shows momentum, which is more motivating than a static number.

Maka Economy — MakaScore gauge, badge tier, MakaCoin wallet
Design progression

From paper sketches to five-tab architecture

I explored three navigation models: (1) a hub-and-spoke with Home linking to everything, (2) a side drawer with categorized access, and (3) a persistent bottom tab bar. I chose the bottom tab bar with five tabs (Home, Marketplace, Practitioners, Video Library, Maka Economy) because it gives each product area equal weight while keeping navigation always visible.

The trade-off: five tabs is at the upper limit of what feels manageable — but the product genuinely has five distinct user needs, and hiding any behind a hamburger menu would reduce discoverability.

Paper sketches — three layout concepts explored

The biggest evolution between wireframes and final was the Marketplace. Early wireframes treated it as a flat product grid, which felt like a generic e-commerce page. The final design introduced categorization, recommendation percentages, and prominent MakaCoin pricing — transforming it from "a store" into "your personalized health toolkit." This shift came directly from research: users wanted curation, not selection.

Visual direction

Dark theme — premium, not clinical

The app uses a dark UI with teal/mint accent colors, warm gradient backgrounds on cards, and a clean card-based layout. This is unusual for health apps, which typically default to clinical whites and blues.

Maka Health wanted to position itself as premium and modern — closer to a fintech app than a clinical tool. I supported this direction because the research showed our target users associated white/clinical interfaces with "being sick." The dark background also makes the teal accent and card surfaces pop, creating clear visual hierarchy. In usability testing, no participants flagged the dark theme as unusual — several said it felt "sleek" and "different from every other health app."

Color palette — dark theme with teal, purple, and gold accents
Character illustrations used for wayfinding and personality
Testing

What worked and what I changed

I conducted usability testing with 5 participants across the target demographic. Three core task flows were tested: find and book a practitioner consultation, start a health Route from the Home screen, and check MakaCoin balance and understand how to earn more.

What worked
100% booking task completion
Participants valued the match percentage and free consultation option. One said: "The free call makes me feel like they're not just trying to take my money."
Changed
Maka Economy was confusing
Two participants didn't understand MakaScore vs MakaCoin. I added "?" tooltip icons next to each label — small affordances that let curious users learn without cluttering the interface.
Changed
Route discoverability was low
Participants scrolled past Routes treating them as decoration. I increased visual weight and added clearer action language to signal these were interactive, not informational.
Changed
Marketplace filters reordered
Original filter chips didn't match how participants thought about supplements. Reordered by priority: Recommended first (personalized), then Specials (value), then Trending (social proof).

I presented research findings, design rationale, and usability results to the product lead and development team. The key tension: the dev team questioned whether five tabs was technically feasible for v1. The data — particularly the interview finding about users mentally separating fitness, nutrition, and consultations — persuaded the team to keep the full five-tab structure.

Reflection

What this project taught me

5
Distinct product areas designed as one cohesive ecosystem
40+
Research participants across questionnaire and interviews
4wk
End-to-end from research to delivered prototype

Designing an ecosystem is different from designing a feature. Five product areas had to share navigation patterns, visual language, and the MakaCoin economy without any single area feeling bolted on. I learned that design systems aren't just about consistency — they're about making complexity feel simple.

Health optimization ≠ healthcare. Early in the project, I kept referencing clinical health apps for inspiration. That was wrong. Maka Health's users aren't sick — they're proactively investing in themselves. This realization shifted my entire approach to tone, visual design, and interaction patterns.

Test earlier, test cheaper. I wish I had tested paper prototypes with real users instead of saving testing for the high-fidelity stage. The Route discoverability issue and MakaScore confusion could have been caught earlier with simple sketches shown to 3 people.

Advocate with evidence. The calendar-first Home screen was not the team's initial preference — they wanted to showcase the MakaScore. Research gave me the confidence to push back respectfully. This was the first time I used data to change a team's direction, and it taught me that the designer's job isn't to have the best ideas — it's to bring the user's perspective into the room with evidence.
