Plowguys

Redesigning a crew member app for snow removal workers — where thick gloves, harsh weather, and split-second task management demanded an interface built for the field, not the office.

Decisive Insight

The app wasn't built for gloved hands in a snowstorm. Crew members needed fewer decisions, larger targets, and voice-first interaction — not more features.

Plowguys' existing crew app suffered from a classic mismatch: it was designed as a standard task management tool, but used by workers in extreme physical conditions. Crew members wearing thick insulated gloves struggled with small touch targets. Hidden menus buried critical trip information. Confusing iconography forced cognitive effort in moments that demanded speed. The problem wasn't missing functionality — it was operational usability under constraint.

01 — Problem

A task app that fights its own users

Plowguys is a snow removal service where route managers assign cleanup routes to crew members during and after storms. The crew member app is the primary tool for receiving assignments, navigating properties, checking in/out of jobs, and tracking task completion.

The existing app had critical usability issues: mismatched iconography, buried navigation, and an interaction model that assumed clean, dry hands and full attention. In reality, crew members operated the app in freezing temperatures, wearing thick gloves, often while transitioning between properties. Every moment of confusion meant delayed service.

02 — Evidence

Heuristic audit + field feedback

The UX audit evaluated the existing app against Nielsen's usability heuristics. Simultaneously, direct feedback from crew members revealed the physical constraints that no heuristic checklist would capture on its own.

Heuristic 01 — Match Between System & Real World

Play icon misread as audio control

The primary action button used a play icon — universally associated with audio/video playback. Crew members (and the designer) initially misread it as a "listen" control rather than a route-start trigger. The metaphor didn't match the mental model of starting a snow removal task.

Heuristic 02 — Consistency & Standards

Oversized arrow broke visual hierarchy

A disproportionately large arrow button dominated the home screen, creating an unbalanced visual hierarchy. It drew attention away from the actual task information crew members needed to scan quickly upon opening the app.

Heuristic 03 — Recognition Rather Than Recall

Trip summaries hidden in hamburger menu

Critical upcoming trip information was buried inside a hamburger menu — requiring crew members to remember it existed and navigate to find it. This increased cognitive load precisely when users needed a quick, glanceable overview before heading out into the storm.

"We wear thick gloves out there. Sometimes I can't even unlock my phone, let alone tap a tiny button to check into a property." — Crew Member Feedback
"Before the busy season hits, I just want to open the app and see what's coming up. I don't want to dig through menus." — Crew Member Feedback
Field Finding 01

Gloved interaction is the norm, not the exception

Crew members wear thick insulated gloves throughout their shifts. Small touch targets, swipe gestures, and precision taps are functionally impossible. The interface needed to be operable with a gloved thumb — or not at all.

Field Finding 02

Voice recognition: unanimous preference

When surveyed on preferred interaction method — voice recognition vs. remote control — 100% of crew members chose voice. This wasn't a nice-to-have; it was the only viable hands-free option for their working conditions.

Field Finding 03

Pre-shift overview is the #1 need

As the busy season approached, crew members' top request was a fast, at-a-glance trip summary. They needed to know how many properties, which services, and total estimated time — without any navigation required.

Competitive Insight

Map-heavy apps set the bar

Analysis of overlapping apps — ride-hailing (Uber-style activity views), health tracking (Noom's task flow), and delivery services (Foodpanda's order status) — revealed best practices for sequential task management, real-time status indicators, and map-centered navigation that Plowguys was missing.

03 — User Context

Two roles, one app

The crew member app sits in a larger ecosystem. Route managers assign routes; crew members execute them. Understanding this relationship clarified that the app's primary job was to minimize the gap between assignment and action — getting crew members to the right property, doing the right task, as fast as possible.

Context A — In-Transit

Between Properties

"Just tell me where to go next."
The crew member is moving between properties. They need the next address, navigation, and a one-tap check-in. Anything that requires reading, scrolling, or multi-step interaction is a blocker. Voice commands and large map displays are essential here.

Context B — On-Site

"What exactly do I need to do here?"
The crew member has arrived and needs to know which services this property requires — sidewalk, deicing, roof clearing, walkway. They need to check tasks off, report issues, take photos, and check out. Clarity and speed are paramount; ambient information (map, other properties) should recede.

04 — Design Decision

Restructure, surface, enlarge

Three design moves, each traced directly to audit findings and field feedback. The redesign wasn't about adding features — it was about restructuring what already existed so it could be used under real-world constraints.

ChangeProblem → Solution
Bottom Navigation Settings, trip info, and categories were buried in a hamburger menu → Reorganized into a persistent 5-tab bottom bar (Route, Calendar, Trips, Chat, Settings) for thumb-reachable, glove-friendly access.
Trip Summary Card No pre-shift overview available at a glance → Prominent trip summary showing property count, total estimated time, and service types — visible immediately on the Trips tab without any navigation.
Map + Task Integration Map and task list were disconnected → Unified view with interactive map on top and scrollable property/task list below, with numbered property markers matching the list sequence.
Enlarged Touch Targets Small buttons and icons unusable with gloves → Increased all primary CTAs (Check In, Check Out, Pause) to meet minimum 48px tap targets, with generous spacing between interactive elements.
Voice Recognition All interactions required touch input → Integrated voice command support for check-in, check-out, and status updates — enabling fully hands-free operation during transit and on-site work.

Trade-offs Considered

What we prioritized

Operational speed over information density. Bottom navigation over hamburger menu despite screen space cost. Voice-first interaction for core actions. Seasonal-adaptive UI themes (winter "On" mode) to match the work context. Glanceable trip summaries over detailed scheduling views.

What we deprioritized

Complex route optimization features — valuable but out of scope for the crew member's needs (this is the route manager's domain). Real-time crew-to-crew communication beyond basic chat. Sign language accessibility and new route suggestion features were flagged for future iterations, not MVP.

05 — Process

From audit to prototype in three phases

Phase 01

Reorganization

Restructured the information architecture. Elevated hidden categories into persistent bottom navigation. Made trip summaries and route info immediately accessible on app launch.

Phase 02

Wireframes

Designed low-fidelity wireframes with seasonal context — the "On" season (winter) UI featured prominent task checklists, map-integrated route views, and enlarged interactive elements.

Phase 03

Prototype

Built an interactive prototype covering the full trip flow: route overview → navigate to property → check in → complete tasks → photo documentation → check out → next property. Tested against original heuristic violations.

Outcome

Redesigned app shipped for crew testing

The redesigned crew member app addressed all three heuristic violations from the audit, introduced voice-first interaction for gloved operation, and surfaced critical trip information that was previously buried. The prototype was delivered to Plowguys for crew field testing ahead of the next snow season.

06 — Next Iteration

What comes next

The redesign established the structural foundation. Future iterations will gather field user reviews and pain points from real storm-season use, focus on communication flows between crew members and route managers, and revisit earlier design explorations for features like new route suggestions and sign language accessibility.

The biggest open question remains validation: does the voice-first approach hold up when crew members are operating noisy snow plows? Field testing will answer this and inform whether voice commands need a fallback interaction layer beyond enlarged touch targets.

07 — Reflection

What I learned

This project taught me that usability heuristics are necessary but not sufficient. The heuristic audit caught interface-level problems — wrong icons, hidden menus, visual imbalance. But the most impactful insight came from understanding the physical context of use: gloved hands, moving vehicles, harsh weather. No heuristic checklist surfaces that; you have to talk to the people doing the work.

Competitive analysis across adjacent domains (ride-hailing, health tracking, food delivery) proved more valuable than looking at direct competitors. Plowguys doesn't compete with other snow removal apps — it competes with every map-heavy, task-sequential mobile experience its crew members have ever used. That's the bar.
