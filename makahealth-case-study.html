03 — Ship in Constraints

Makahealth Patient Portal

Patients didn't need more health data — they needed a single trusted view that connected scattered records into one actionable summary. We scoped down to that core and shipped it in 12 weeks.

The problem

Patients couldn't see their own health story

Makahealth was building a patient-facing portal to help people manage personal health data — lab results, medications, appointments, provider notes. But existing health apps treated each data type as a separate silo. Patients were expected to navigate between tabs and dashboards to piece together their own health picture.

The company's initial brief asked for a comprehensive portal with feature parity to competitor apps. The question was whether building more features was the right approach — or whether patients needed something fundamentally different.

Competitive landscape — existing health portals
Evidence

Three signals pointed to the same gap

Rather than walking through each research method sequentially, here's what mattered: three independent sources converged on the same finding.

Competitive Analysis
Feature overload, no synthesis
Analyzed HealthTap, Healow, and 3 other patient portals. All offered raw data access (labs, meds, vitals) but none synthesized it into a unified health narrative. Patients could see individual trees but never the forest.
User Research
Patients wanted context, not data
Surveyed and interviewed patients about their health tracking habits. The recurring theme: "I can see my numbers, but I don't know what they mean together." They wanted their health story told to them, not handed to them in fragments.
Affinity Mapping
Synthesis over access
Clustered 120+ data points from research. The dominant cluster wasn't about missing features — it was about missing connections between existing data. Users needed a connective layer, not more endpoints.
Stakeholder Input
Clinical team confirmed the pattern
Clinical advisors noted that patients who understood the relationship between their medications, lab results, and lifestyle changes had better adherence rates. The product opportunity was in making those relationships visible.

The decision

One unified health summary instead of five separate dashboards

Instead of building feature parity with competitors (separate tabs for labs, medications, appointments, vitals, and notes), we designed a single summary view that connected data points into a patient-readable health narrative. One screen, not five.

This meant cutting planned features — the recipe summarizer, detailed vitals history, and appointment scheduling were deprioritized. The core bet: a simpler product that patients actually understood would outperform a comprehensive one they bounced from.

What we gained
Unified health summary patients could read in under 60 seconds
Clear data relationships (labs ↔ medications ↔ lifestyle)
Shipped within the 12-week internship window
Reduced cognitive load for first-time users
What we sacrificed
Recipe summarizer feature (deprioritized to v2)
Detailed vitals history and trend charts
In-app appointment scheduling
Feature parity with competitor portals
Core design — unified health summary screen
Design language

Warm, approachable, clinically trustworthy

Health apps tend toward clinical blues and sterile whites. We chose a warmer palette with the Makahealth brand colors — lively greens and friendly character illustrations — to reduce the anxiety patients feel when viewing health data. The visual tone says "this is approachable" while the information hierarchy says "this is trustworthy."

This project taught me that the hardest design decision isn't what to add — it's what to cut. Scoping down to one core view felt risky during the process, but it's what made shipping possible and the product coherent. As a first internship, it also showed me how to navigate real stakeholder dynamics: the clinical team wanted comprehensive data access, the engineering team wanted feasible scope, and patients wanted simplicity. The unified summary was the design that balanced all three.
